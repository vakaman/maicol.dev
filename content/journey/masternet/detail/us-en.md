# A few steps back

Before I start talking about my journey at Masternet, I want to tell a bit of my story before I got there. For a while, after teaching at Maxper and GP Informática, I was unemployed, but I did not stay still. My former brother-in-law had a small wireless internet provider and served a few customers in the region, something around 150 clients or so. Our deal was simple: I would help with installations, and he would help me keep the house running. He was already living with me and my brothers, and we had to figure things out somehow.

He worked really well, was very sharp and intelligent, and I held on to that. I needed to learn everything he had to show me, and that is how it went for quite a while. Back then, our "competitor" was Masternet, and I saw it as a giant company, with several installers, transmission towers across the city, and an enviable reputation.

With that in mind, and knowing that side job would not last forever, I started wanting to work at Masternet. So I went there for an interview, full of drive and sure that I had the potential to do a good job. I did not get approved because I did not have a driver's license, and the only opening at the time was for an installer. I left pretty frustrated, but with one clear goal in mind: get my driver's license.

In the end, that interview I failed was one of the best things that happened to me, because it forced me to get my license and gave me time to gain experience as a networking and development professional. Before Master, I ended up working at L&C through my former brother-in-law's recommendation. There I learned a lot about Mikrotik, networking in general, and systems development.

# Second attempt

After about a year working at L&C, and after building solid experience with Mikrotik and with the development of Agenda, I went back for another interview. This time I already had a driver's license, but I was not aiming to be an installer. I got there full of drive and with a very clear goal: get hired.

Fortunately, my previous experience had prepared me well, and I already had enough knowledge to be persuasive. Some interview time, many questions, tests and... I made it. I was officially hired. Where? At Masternet, the place that was truly a reference for me. I was happy and proud. This is where my new journey as a technical support attendant begins.

# Welcome to Masternet

I started at Master with a feeling of a fresh start. I had gotten married not long before, rented a house and, besides the responsibility of work, the responsibility of supporting a family was on my shoulders. Just like at L&C, I started in support, and unlike before, I did not need to unblock or block customers, sell plans or even install in some situations. My job was just... to serve customers and provide support.

Up to a point, I was fine with that "just serving customers," but before long I started realizing I could contribute more to the company. Between one call and another, I had a lot of free time, and I did not want to spend it consuming useless content on the internet. Because of that, I asked my manager if I could use that free time to study and develop a system for Master.

At that time, we used Excel, email, and a giant archive with all the company's physical contracts. Every time a call ended with the conclusion that we needed to visit the client, I had to get their contract to know whether the equipment was on loan or not. That meant going to the building next door, climbing two or three flights of stairs, going to finance, opening the archive, finding the contract and, only then, discovering whether the equipment was on loan or not. At first, fine, but by the twentieth time, nobody deserves that.

My goal was to have a simple system, like before, where the user could log in, search for the client and find their scanned contract. Simple as that.

# The beginning of Agenda

As soon as I got permission to start studying and building it, I quickly began with the login, a basic interface with user lookup, a panel with client data and... a button to view contracts. I built everything on my own machine. At the time, I had no idea what Docker, Linux, server, or client really meant. I knew the basics, and that was enough to handle the complexity of starting a simple system for a small number of users.

Just a few weeks later, there it was: v0. A login with search and a client panel, with a place where contracts could be viewed. I presented the solution and it was approved immediately. The gain from using the tool was clear. Very quickly, my boss asked the company to shift efforts toward feeding the system database, registering the 2,000 customers we had at the time, scanning their contracts and uploading everything into the system. Now it was easy to see customer data, and there was no need for all that effort searching physical files anymore. That was the beginning of the company's modernization era.

# Earning trust

With Agenda evolving fast and my daily work under control, I kept earning more and more of my managers' trust. I simply automated and built solutions for everything. Customer onboarding flow with plan selection, installation scheduling, maintenance, address change, equipment pickup. For every process in the company, I got close to the professional with the most experience in that flow, understood what was being done, designed a solution and, in a matter of days, brought a new Agenda feature that made the work simple and objective.

I went through basically every department: finance, inventory, support, sales. People trusted me and trusted my work.

# Next steps

Remember Mikrotik? Well, the whole company used that system, and both network and customer management were handled with it. I never saw myself as only a developer or only a network engineer, because since the beginning of my career I have always been involved with both, almost like a symbiosis.

My networking knowledge grew, just like Agenda did, and besides studying systems development, I started studying networks as well. That was when I started earning Mikrotik certifications, MTCNA, MTCRE, and others came naturally after that. To me, it was exciting and fun. Over time, I became the technical lead for the company's network infrastructure.

# From support attendant to technical lead?

I stayed at Master for many years. What started simple became robust, complex and ready for the company's growth. Phone system with user identification, automated blocks and unblocks, heatmaps for purchase intent. I simply did everything, both from the systems side and from the network side.

I built a 100% routed network, using OSPF and BGP with MPLS to control network routes. I installed L2/L3 switches, Huawei routers, Huawei OLTs, firewalls, DNS servers and set up a few data centers, stacking servers for redundancy, with battery banks and cooling. I was constantly involved in network training, many from NIC.br, and some at Datacom, where I made many friends. In short... I was in heaven.

You may be asking yourself: did you really do all that? And... yes, I was in love with it. For me, having that level of control over everything that was helping the company grow was beautiful. I was responsible for basically the entire technological infrastructure of the company. I installed every Linux server that ran there, maintained all the necessary databases, the hypervisors such as Citrix, Proxmox, Hyper-V and others. I had full control over the VMs, restrictions, security, and everything else you can imagine.

# I need help

Time passed, the responsibility was huge, and I needed to create room for all the initiatives I was pushing forward. Because of that, I started training people so they could handle and evolve the solutions I had built, both in systems development and in server and network infrastructure.

Not everyone wanted to learn that, many wanted to stay where they were, and that was fine by me. I am happy that I managed to bring some people into development and networking, and today they are excellent professionals and friends. Help was necessary at that moment, and I had already reached a point where it was no longer possible to keep everything going by myself.

Reinforcements arrived at the right time, seriously... I was losing it. My daytime responsibilities never stopped, while at times the late night kept calling me. That was where I discovered there is something called firmware and that equipment also suffers from bugs and needs updates. Piles and piles of release notes coming out, and many times I needed a fix that came in release x or y. But since money is not infinite, I could not get enough investment to have redundancy for 100% of the equipment, and firmware updates had to be done in the middle of the night.

# Thanks

Today I sincerely thank all the professionals who helped me during that time: Douglas Medeiros, Jackson Mafra, Douglas Mombach, Gabriel Souza, Samuel Sublate and many others. You are incredible, both professionally and as friends. I am very happy to have had the chance to meet you.

# Time to say goodbye

After many years, with a robust structure ready for many more years of growth, it was time to sail toward new waters. I joined Master when it had around 200 Mb of aggregate network consumption, and when I left it was already over 20 Gb during peak hours. The network could handle proportionally much more, the system was capable of dealing with the complexity of many users, the professionals there were trained and ready to handle whatever came next and, in the end... I was no longer needed there.

I felt obligated to face new battles in environments I did not yet dominate. That is how I said goodbye to Masternet.
